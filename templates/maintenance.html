Maintain Body Weight

Maintaining a stable body weight means keeping your body weight consistent by balancing how much you eat with how much energy you use. To do this, you should eat around your maintenance calories, which are the calories your body needs to function daily without gaining or losing weight. Meals should be balanced and include adequate protein to maintain muscle, carbohydrates for daily energy, and healthy fats for hormone and overall health. Eating regular meals, usually 3 main meals and 1–2 small snacks, helps control hunger and prevents overeating. Exercise should be done regularly, with strength training 2–4 times a week to preserve muscle mass and cardio activities like walking, cycling, or jogging for 20–30 minutes on most days to support heart health and calorie balance. Staying active throughout the day, such as walking more and avoiding long sitting hours, also helps maintain weight. Getting 7–9 hours of sleep is important because poor sleep can increase hunger and cravings. Drinking enough water supports digestion and helps control appetite. Avoid extreme dieting, skipping meals, or overeating on weekends, as these habits can disturb weight balance. Consistency is the key—small, healthy habits followed daily are more effective than strict short-term plans. By eating mindfully, exercising regularly, managing stress, and maintaining a balanced lifestyle, a stable and healthy body weight can be maintained over time.

Calorie Balance
  • Calories are the energy your body needs to function daily
  • Eating at maintenance calories helps keep body weight stable
  • Too many calories cause weight gain, too few cause weight loss
  • Calories should come from nutritious, whole foods
Macronutrient Balance
  • Protein: 1.6–2.2 g/kg body weight to maintain muscle mass
  • Protein helps maintain and repair muscles
  • Adequate protein prevents muscle loss
Carbohydrates & Fats
  • 45–55% of total calories for energy
  • Carbs provide energy for daily activities and exercise
  • Choose complex carbs like whole grains, fruits, and vegetables
  • 20–30% of total calories for hormone and overall health
  • Healthy fats support hormone production and brain function
  • Include sources like nuts, seeds, avocados, and olive oil
